PET

Your portal to mastering the exam

Overview

PET, or Cambridge Preliminary English Test, is a 2 hour and 20 minute exam for intermediate English language learners seeking to certify themselves at the B1 CEFR Level. It is one of the most popular choices in the world.

The certification is an indication to many employers and schools that you are a competent user of English. Some universities even offer credit in place of an introductory English course.

It is also provides good preparation for other language exams you may take in the future, especially IELTS.

PET is divided into 4 different exams:

  • Reading
  • Writing
  • Listening
  • Speaking

For a detailed description of the types of questions in each part of each exam, visit the bottom half of this page.

Strategies

In order to pass the PET exam, or any English certification exam for that matter, you should invest substantial effort into improving your English in general. Many students who only study the exam fail because they never developed a foundation to work with. Many others pass, even with decent results, however they discover later on that they are unable to communicate in the real world, as their competencies are limited to nothing but an exam.

You should spend just as much time improving your overall English as you do on the exam itself. Visit the is page for resources on improving.

Fortunately it is much easier and more enjoyable for most learners to work on the English in general, as opposed to constantly fighting their way through boring textbook exercises. Because of this, I have devised a strategy:

In the beginning of your studies, devote most of your time to general learning and limit your PET-specific study time to just a few tasks: sampling the exam, taking a guided exam, and creating an exam journal. Try to have fun studying by exploring what material you enjoy. Some students enjoy watching Netflix, others read the news. The trick is to find something in English that you actually want to read or watch or listen to.

As time goes on, you should gradually reduce the amount of time spent on general learning and increase that dedicated to the exam itself. This works out well as you take simulations throughout your course to determine your strengths and weaknesses, thus starting at the root of the problem.

Various websites provide free practice questions for each part of the Reading and Listening exams. After your first few simulations, you will know which questions you'll need to practice.

Reading

  • Time: 1 hour 30 minutes (includes the Writing Exam)
  • Sections: 5
  • Questions: 35

Writing

  • Time: 1 hour 30 minutes (includes the Reading Exam)
  • Sections: 3
  • Questions: 7 (2 are essay questions)
Free practice on ExamEnglish.net Free practice on Flo-Joe

Listening

  • Time: 36 minutes
  • Sections: 4
  • Questions: 25
Free practice on ExamEnglish.net Free practice on Flo-Joe

Speaking

  • Time: 10-12 minutes
  • Sections: 4
  • Questions: Minimum 10 per student
